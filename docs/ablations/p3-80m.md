# Phase 3: 77M scale-up — does scale help? (2026-06-20)

GPU scale-up on a rented ThunderCompute A6000 (per
`docs/superpowers/specs/2026-06-20-chess-rl-phase3-gpu-scaleup-design.md`). The
question: **does a 4× bigger model, trained on the identical data, actually play
better with search?** Answered with a clean controlled experiment — a 77M model
vs a 10M control, both trained on the **same ~100M dense positions**, compared
**head-to-head at equal MCTS budget** (Phase-2's lesson: games, not puzzles, are
the strength metric).

## Setup

Both models trained on the **same** ~100M dense positions (`prdev/chessbench-full-policy-value`,
T=0.1; 100,271,212 train / 201,045 val), **25,000 steps**, batch 1024, EMA 0.999,
bf16 AMP, on one A6000. One pass over the data (zero repeats) via the new
streaming dataloader (the eager loader OOMs at this scale).

| model | preset | params | dims | lr |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **77M** | `p3-80m` | 76,982,212 | d512 / 16L / 16h / d_ff3072, SwiGLU, HL-Gauss-64, dropout 0.05 | 2e-4 |
| **10M control** | `p2-value-swiglu-drop` | 10,250,052 | d256 / 8L / 8h / d_ff1024, SwiGLU, HL-Gauss-64, dropout 0.05 | 3e-4 |

## Primary result — head-to-head (the scale test)

`arena_eval.py --vs`, MCTS 50 sims, 60 games from 8-ply random openings
(deterministic engines need opening diversity), colour-balanced:

> **77M vs 10M control: 19W / 39D / 2L → score 0.642, ≈ +101 Elo.**

Decisive games **19–2** to the 77M; only 2 losses in 60. **Scale helps in actual
play.** The bigger model, on identical data, is meaningfully stronger with search.

## Training metrics (final, step 25k, on the 100M dense val)

| model | val_policy_loss | val_top1 | value sign-acc | train wall |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 77M | **2.762** | **0.493** | **0.934** | 6.6 h |
| 10M control | 2.787 | 0.449 | 0.926 | 1.6 h |

On **general (dense) positions** the 77M leads on both policy (0.493 vs 0.449)
and value (0.934 vs 0.926). Consistent with the head-to-head: scale helps.

## Puzzle gates (`ablate.py --fast`, tactical Lichess puzzles)

| model | raw_top1@300 | raw_top1@1000 | mate-in-1 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 77M | 0.290 | 0.320 | **3/3** |
| 10M control | 0.300 | 0.328 | 2/3 |
| *(ref) Phase-2 p2-value (10M, 300k data)* | — | *0.375* | *2/3* |
| *(ref) baseline-v1* | — | *0.289* | *2/3* |

**On tactical puzzles the 77M is NOT better than the 10M (0.32 vs 0.328), and
both are below Phase-2's p2-value (0.375).** Yet the 77M wins the head-to-head and
leads on dense val. This re-confirms the Phase-2 finding: **puzzle top-1 misjudges
engine strength.** The puzzle dip vs Phase-2 most likely reflects the **one-pass
over-100M regime** (vs many epochs over 300k, which over-fits the narrow puzzle
slice) plus the known **`value_loss_weight=1.0` throttle** on policy (the
64-bucket HL-Gauss CE dominates the loss). The 77M does win mate-in-1 (3/3).

## Verdict

**The primary gate passes: scale helps.** 77M beats the 10M control +101 Elo
head-to-head at equal MCTS, on identical data — a clean, real result at our
budget. It shows up in *general play* (dense val) and *games*, not in *tactical
puzzles*, exactly as the puzzles-aren't-strength lesson predicts. The companion
**data-lever ablation (below) is even larger: +223 Elo** from the 330× bigger
data pool at fixed 10M arch — so **data > scale** here, and the bulk of the
Phase-2→Phase-3 gain is data, not capacity.

This does **not** mean we're near Stockfish — it means the architecture+recipe
scales, which is the question Phase 3 was built to answer. Honest ceiling
unchanged: a strong neural engine at ~1 epoch over 100M, not a teacher-beater
(that needs the self-play loop + far more data; see
`docs/codex_comments/sota_elo_architecture_recommendations.md`).

## Caveats & next

- **Data/compute-bound, not capacity-bound.** 25k steps ≈ 1 pass over 100M (25M
  positions of compute). Val loss had flattened but the model is under-trained
  for its size — more steps / more data is the lever (snapshot `chess-rl-p3-100m-data`
  preserves the 100M + env for a longer run).
- **`value_loss_weight=0.3` experiment: DONE** (see section below). Fresh 25k A/B
  (not warm-start, for a clean single-variable comparison). Confirms the throttle
  on policy top-1 but the engine gain is within noise — details below.
- **Absolute-Elo Stockfish ladder: DONE** (see section below) — MCTS engine
  ~2000 Elo, undefeated vs SF skills 6/8/10 (depth 4), +340 over raw. Only the
  secondary head-to-head vs the prior champion `p2_full_300k` (confounded by
  data, hence secondary) remains unrun.
## Value & dense-policy metrics (Phase-0 dense val bag, 62k)

| metric | 77M | 10M control |
| --- | --- | --- |
| dense top1 / top3 / top5 | **0.468 / 0.751 / 0.852** | 0.424 / 0.701 / 0.810 |
| policy CE | 3.057 | 3.074 |
| value sign-acc | **0.923** | 0.906 |
| WDL cross-entropy | **1.636** | 1.699 |
| draw calibration (|pred−actual|, lower=better) | **0.015** | 0.017 |
| legal mass | 0.999 | 0.998 |

The 77M's **value head is better-calibrated** (lower WDL-CE, better draw
calibration, higher sign-acc) and its policy is better top-1/3/5. Better value
calibration is the mechanism behind the head-to-head win — MCTS leans on leaf
values, and the 77M's are sharper. This is the metric the Codex memo flagged as
the real predictor of search strength, and it lines up with the +101 Elo.

## Absolute-Elo Stockfish ladder (77M vs Stockfish depth-4)

`arena_eval.py --skills 6 8 10`, 16 games/rung, MCTS 50 sims, SF depth 4, on
local MPS (free — no GPU spend). `estElo` = the rung's nominal Elo + Elo-from-score.

| mover | vs SF skill (≈Elo) | W/D/L | score | est Elo |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| raw | 6 (1400) | 10/6/0 | 0.812 | ~1655 |
| **mcts** | 6 (1400) | **15/1/0** | **0.969** | **~1997** |
| raw | 8 (1600) | 4/10/2 | 0.562 | ~1644 |
| **mcts** | 8 (1600) | **13/3/0** | **0.906** | **~1994** |
| raw | 10 (1800) | 3/11/2 | 0.531 | ~1822 |
| **mcts** | 10 (1800) | **9/7/0** | **0.781** | **~2021** |

**The MCTS engine is undefeated across the whole ladder — zero losses at skills
6, 8 and 10** — holding a remarkably stable **~2000 est Elo** (1997 / 1994 /
2021). It dominates skill-6 (15W/1D/0L) and still scores 0.78 against skill-10,
so **~2000 is a floor, not a ceiling** (the ladder ran out of rungs before the
engine ran out of strength; higher skills would be needed to find where it tops
out). Search adds **~+340 Elo over the raw policy** (raw plateaus ~1650–1820 and
gets outclassed) — the clean inverse of the Phase-2 10M regime where raw ≥ MCTS
on puzzles. **At 77M scale, MCTS is decisively the engine**, confirming the
head-to-head and value-calibration story.

*Caveat:* depth-4 Stockfish is shallow, so these absolute numbers are **rough and
likely optimistic** vs a full-search SF ladder — the skill→Elo labels assume
normal depth. The robust, depth-independent signals are the *shape*: MCTS ≫ raw,
MCTS undefeated, and ~2000 stable across rungs.

## Data-lever ablation — data > scale at this budget

Companion to the scale test. Same `arena_eval.py --vs` harness (MCTS 50 sims, 60
games, 8-ply random openings), but holding the **architecture fixed at 10M** and
changing only the **training data**: the 10M-control (this phase's ~100M-position
pool) vs `p2_full_300k` (the Phase-2 champion, identical `p2-value-swiglu-drop`
arch, trained on the 300k pool).

> **10M-control vs `p2_full_300k`: 34W / 26D / 0L → score 0.783, ≈ +223 Elo.**

Undefeated in 60 games. Putting the two ablations side by side, **at the same MCTS
budget on the same harness:**

| lever | comparison (fixed other axis) | Elo gain |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **data** | 10M on 100M  vs  10M on 300k | **+223** |
| scale | 77M on 100M  vs  10M on 100M | +101 |

**Data is the bigger lever** — more than double the scale gain. Most of the
Phase-2→Phase-3 improvement came from the ~330× larger data pool, not from the
4× larger model. This is the cleanest possible confirmation of the headline
conclusion: at our compute budget the engine is **data/compute-bound, not
capacity-bound** — the lever is more (unique) data and more training, with model
size a secondary multiplier. (Caveat: changing the data pool also changes the
training schedule, so this is "Phase-3 data+schedule vs Phase-2 data+schedule" at
fixed arch; the ~330× data increase dominates that bundle.)

## vlw=0.3 ablation — policy throttle confirmed, engine unchanged

The deferred follow-up: does down-weighting the value loss (1.0 → 0.3) free
policy capacity? Fresh 25k-step run, **identical to p3_80m except
`--value-loss-weight 0.3`** — the vlw=1.0 control (p3_80m) already exists, so this
is a clean single-variable A/B, not a warm-start. Same 100M data, A100, bf16 AMP.

| metric (final 25k, 100M dense val) | vlw=1.0 (p3_80m) | **vlw=0.3** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| val_top1 (dense policy) | 0.4927 | **0.506** |
| val_policy_loss | 2.762 | **2.752** |
| value sign-acc | 0.934 | 0.933 |

**Policy throttle confirmed:** vlw=0.3 passes vlw=1.0's 25k-final top1 (0.493) by
~step 20k and finishes at **0.506**, at **no value sign-acc cost** (0.933 ≈
0.934). The 64-bucket HL-Gauss value CE at weight 1.0 *was* suppressing policy
learning, exactly as suspected.

**But the engine is unchanged.** Head-to-head, same harness (MCTS 50 sims, 60
games, 8-ply random openings):

> **vlw=0.3 vs p3_80m (vlw=1.0): 10W / 43D / 7L → 0.525, +17 Elo.**

+17 over 60 games is **within noise** (±~45 Elo; 43/60 drawn). And on tactical
puzzles vlw=0.3 is actually slightly *lower* (raw_top1@1000 0.306 vs 0.320, mate
2/3 vs 3/3). So three policy/strength metrics rank three different ways:

| metric | dense val_top1 | puzzle raw_top1 | engine (head-to-head) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| winner | **vlw=0.3** | vlw=1.0 | ~tie (+17, noise) |

**Verdict: hypothesis confirmed on policy metrics, but the engine gain is
negligible.** Down-weighting value genuinely lifts dense policy top-1, but with
the value head tied and MCTS smoothing over small policy differences, it doesn't
move engine strength. The same lesson a third time: at this scale the engine is
**value/search- and data-bound, not policy-top1-bound**. vlw=0.3 is a small,
free policy-quality gain with no engine downside — a reasonable default going
forward, but **not a lever**. The levers remain *more data* and *self-play*.
